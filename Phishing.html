What is phishing?

Phishing is a type of cyberattack that uses fraudulent emails, text messages, phone calls or websites to trick people into sharing sensitive data, downloading malware or otherwise exposing themselves to cybercrime.

Types of phishing attacks

  • Bulk email phishing
  • Spear phishing
  • Business email compromise (BEC)

Why phishing is a major cyberthreat

Phishing is popular among cybercriminals and highly effective. According to IBM's Cost of a Data Breach report, phishing is the most common data breach vector, accounting for 15% of all breaches. Breaches caused by phishing cost organizations an average of USD 4.88 million.

Phishers can be lone scammers or sophisticated criminal gangs. They can use phishing for many malicious ends, including identity theft, credit card fraud, monetary theft, extortion, account takeovers, espionage and more.

Bulk email phishing

In bulk email phishing, scammers indiscriminately send spam emails to as many people as possible, hoping that a fraction of the targets fall for the attack.

  • Impersonation of Trusted Brands – Scammers mimic well-known businesses using logos, branding, and spoofed email addresses to trick recipients into believing the email is legitimate.
  • Urgency and Emotional Manipulation – Phishing emails often use urgent subject lines like "Payment Issue" or "Account Suspended" to pressure recipients into acting quickly without verifying authenticity.
  • Malicious Links and Attachments – Emails may contain links leading to fake login pages that steal credentials or attachments that install malware on the victim’s device.

Spear phishing

Spear phishing is a targeted phishing attack on a specific individual. The target is usually someone with privileged access to sensitive data or special authority that the scammer can exploit, such as a finance manager who can move money from company accounts.

  • Targeted Personalization – Spear phishers gather personal details from social media and professional networks to craft convincing emails that appear to come from trusted sources.
  • Impersonation of Trusted Contacts – Attackers pose as bosses, coworkers, or vendors, using familiar language and context to manipulate victims into taking harmful actions.
  • Whaling Attacks – High-value individuals, such as executives or wealthy individuals, are specifically targeted in "whale phishing" attacks due to their access to sensitive information and financial assets.

Business email compromise (BEC)

BEC – is a class of spear phishing attacks that attempt to steal money or valuable information—for example, trade secrets, customer data or financial information—from a business or other organization.

BEC attacks can take several forms. Two of the most common include:

  • CEO fraud – The scammer impersonates a C-level executive, often by hijacking the executive's email account. The scammer sends a message to a lower-level employee instructing them to transfer funds to a fraudulent account, make a purchase from a fraudulent vendor or send files to an unauthorized party.
  • Email account compromise (EAC) – The scammer compromises a lower-level employee's email account, such as the account of a manager in finance, sales or research and development. The scammer uses the account to send fraudulent invoices to vendors, instruct other employees to make fraudulent payments or request access to confidential data.
